The Mechanic

Think in chord intervals (I, IV, V), not note letters. The same shape sequence works in any key - you just start the cycle at a different fret. The cycle uses four physical moves on the fretboard:

  • HAMMER add fingers 2 and 3 to the bar position you're already in - the bar stays put, fingers reorganize.
  • SLIDE +2 move the whole shape up two frets - IV to V is always a whole step in major-key diatonic.
  • ROTATE +1 move up one fret and shift to the next shape family - V's hand position becomes the launching pad for the next I.
  • FLIP ring bar to index bar at the same fret - the chord doesn't change, but the geometry now lets fingers 2 and 3 hammer back into an F-shape, landing on the NEXT inversion of I higher up the neck. The cycle restarts in the same key.

Three shape families do all the work:

  • C-shape - one finger bars across strings 4, 3, 2 (index OR ring depending on stage of the cycle). Same fret on all three strings.
  • F-shape - bar at the root area + two fingers reach to higher frets on strings 4 and 2. Used for IV when hammering from the C-shape I.
  • A-shape - three fingers on strings 4, 3, 2 stepping down (ring high, middle middle, index low). Used for the rotated I.

String 1 (A) is muted throughout - the G-C-E top three strings preserve the interval geometry of guitar's D-G-B set, so guitar muscle memory transfers directly.

What This Looks Like Across Instruments

The interval geometry is what transfers - the physical shape adapts to the instrument's tuning.

Guitar (standard EADGBE) - the D, G, B strings have the same interval pattern as ukulele G, C, E. The exact shapes shown here drop straight onto guitar's top three strings, except the bass strings (E, A, low D) are available too for full chord voicings if you don't mute them. CAGED is the full-neck extension of this idea.
Cigar Box (DGBD) and 5-string Banjo (gDGBD) - all majors are a single barre across all 4 strings at the root fret. The cycle simplifies: ROTATE +1 and FLIP still apply, but HAMMER collapses (I and IV share the same fret position differently). The SLIDE +2 from IV to V is the headline mechanic on these instruments.
Mandolin / Mandola (GDAE / CGDA) - all-fifths tuning, so the shapes are different but the IV-to-V slide-up-2 still holds. The triad inversion math is the same; the fingering family needs its own derivation.

The Compound Insight

The same shape pattern repeats up the neck while you stay in one key. The chord names don't change - D stays D, G stays G, A stays A - but each cycle puts them in different inversions higher and higher on the fretboard.

Once the shape pattern is in muscle memory, you've removed the abstraction of note letters entirely. You think in I, IV, V and let the fretboard hand you whichever inversion is closest at hand.
