Triad Inversions

Walk I-IV-V up the neck with three shape pivots. The chord names stay; only the inversion and fret position climb.

Learn more
How this works

Think in chord intervals (I, IV, V), not note letters. The same shape sequence works in any key - you just start at a different fret.

String 1 (A) stays muted throughout. The G-C-E strings (4, 3, 2) preserve the same interval geometry as guitar's D-G-B set, so guitar muscle memory transfers directly.

The cycle through one position is: I in a shape, HAMMER into the IV shape, SLIDE +2 frets to V. Then a rotation or flip lands the next inversion of I one position higher up the neck. After three positions you're one octave up and the cycle repeats.

The 4 operations
  • HAMMER add fingers 2 and 3 to the bar position you're already in - the bar stays put, fingers reorganize.
  • SLIDE +2 move the whole shape up two frets - IV to V is always a whole step in major-key diatonic.
  • ROTATE +1 move up one fret and shift to the next shape family - V's hand position becomes the launching pad for the next I.
  • FLIP ring bar to index bar at the same fret - the chord doesn't change, but the geometry now lets fingers 2 and 3 hammer back into an F-shape, landing on the NEXT inversion of I higher up the neck.
The 3 shape families
  • C-shape - one finger bars across strings 4, 3, 2 (index OR ring depending on stage of the cycle). Same fret on all three strings.
  • F-shape - bar at the root area + two fingers reach to higher frets on strings 4 and 2. Used for IV when hammering from the C-shape I.
  • A-shape - three fingers on strings 4, 3, 2 stepping down (ring high, middle middle, index low). Used for the rotated I.

Cross-instrument transfer

The interval geometry is what transfers - the physical shape adapts to the instrument's tuning.

Guitar (standard EADGBE) - the D, G, B strings (4, 3, 2 - the middle three) have the same interval pattern as ukulele G, C, E. The exact shapes drop straight onto that D-G-B set. Mute strings 1, 5, 6 for the pure three-string voicing, or include the high E and bass strings for fuller chords. CAGED is the full-neck extension.
Cigar Box (DGBD) / 5-string Banjo (gDGBD) - all majors are a single barre across all 4 strings at the root fret. The cycle simplifies: ROTATE +1 and FLIP still apply, but HAMMER collapses. The SLIDE +2 from IV to V is the headline mechanic.
Mandolin / Mandola (GDAE / CGDA) - all-fifths tuning, so the shapes are different but the IV-to-V slide-up-2 still holds. The triad-inversion math is the same; the fingering family needs its own derivation.
The big picture

The same shape pattern repeats up the neck while you stay in one key. Chord names don't change - D stays D, G stays G, A stays A - each cycle just puts them in different inversions higher and higher on the fretboard.

Once the shape pattern is in muscle memory, you've removed the abstraction of note letters entirely. You think in I, IV, V and let the fretboard hand you whichever inversion is closest at hand.
